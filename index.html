How Standardized Testing is Hurting Black and Latino Students

by Leonardo Lopez Carreno

Within the next two months, New York will be conducting its annual standardized tests for students between grades 3-8. Last year, testing was made entirely optional for students, resulting in only 20.5% of students taking the Math test and 21.6% taking the English Language Arts test. This means that throughout late March and April, most grade school students will be taking a standardized test for the first time in two years, since testing was entirely cancelled for the 2019-2020 academic year.

If we review the test results from the ELA test through 2013-2019 and divide them by the studentsâ€™ race, we will notice that, historically, Black and Latino students have scored more prominently in lower levels than Asian and White students. While all groups collectively increased their presence in higher levels throughout the years, the gap between both groups remains. Between 2013 and 2019, White and Asian students went from having a majority in Levels 2 and 3, to 3 and 4, generally improving the performance of the group as a whole. By contrast, Black and Latino students consistently had a majority in Levels 1 and 2, not showing any general progression.
